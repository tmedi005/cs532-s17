   Patriots' Day (officially Patriots' Day in Massachusetts and Wisconsin
   and Patriot's Day in Maine^[21][1]) is an official [22]state holiday
   commemorating the anniversary of the [23]Battles of Lexington and
   Concord, the first battles of the [24]American Revolutionary War. The
   holiday was originally celebrated on April 19, the actual anniversary
   of the battles (fought in 1775).

   Since 1969, it has been observed on the third Monday in April in
   [25]Massachusetts^[26][2] and in [27]Maine,^[28][3] (which [29]until
   1820 was part of Massachusetts). The Monday holiday creates a three-day
   [30]long weekend. It is also the first day of a vacation week for
   public schools in both states and a school holiday for many local
   colleges and universities, both public and private.

   The day is a [31]public school observance day in [32]Wisconsin.^[33][4]
   Florida law also encourages people to celebrate it, though it is not
   treated as a public holiday.^[34][5]

   Observances and re-enactments of the battles occur annually at
   [35]Lexington Green in [36]Lexington, Massachusetts (around 6:00 am)
   and the [37]Old North Bridge in [38]Concord, Massachusetts (around 9:00
   am). In the morning, mounted re-enactors with state police escorts
   retrace the [39]Midnight Rides of [40]Paul Revere and [41]William
   Dawes, calling out warnings the whole way.

   The biggest celebration of Patriots' Day is the [42]Boston Marathon,
   which has been run every Patriots' Day since April 19, 1897 to mark the
   then-recently established holiday, with the race linking the
   [43]Athenian and American struggles for liberty^[44][6] ([45]marathons
   being so named after the Greek [46]Battle of Marathon).

History[[52]edit]

   Patriots' Day was first proclaimed in Massachusetts in 1894 by Gov.
   [53]Frederic T. Greenhalge replacing [54]Fast Day as a public
   holiday.^[55][1] It was established on April 19, commemorating the date
   of the [56]Battles of Lexington and Concord in 1775, and consolidating
   the longstanding municipal observances of Lexington Day and Concord
   Day. It also marked the first bloodshed of the [57]American Civil War
   in the [58]Baltimore riot of 1861, during which four members of the
   Massachusetts militia were slain and 36 injured. The dual
   commemoration, Greenhalge explained, celebrated "the anniversary of the
   birth of liberty and union." In 1938, with the generation that had
   fought in the Civil War largely off the voter rolls, the Massachusetts
   legislature passed a bill establishing the holiday "in commemoration of
   the opening events of the War of the Revolution."^[59][6]

   Maine followed Massachusetts in 1907 and replaced its Fast Day with
   Patriot's Day.^[60][1]

Sporting events[[61]edit]

   The [62]Boston Marathon is run on Patriots' Day every year.^[63][6]
   Therefore sometimes the holiday is referred to as "Marathon
   Monday".^[64][7]

   The [65]Boston Red Sox have been scheduled to play at home in
   [66]Fenway Park on Patriots' Day every year since 1959. The games were
   postponed due to weather in 1959, 1961, 1965, 1967, and 1984, and
   canceled in 1995^[67][8] because of [68]the late start to the season.
   Since 1968 the games have started early, in the morning, around 11:00
   am. The early start to these games usually resulted in the game ending
   just as the marathon is heading through [69]Kenmore Square. However,
   since 2007 the marathon has started between 9:30 am and 10:00 am,
   resulting in the racers going through Kenmore towards the middle of the
   Red Sox game.^[70][9]
